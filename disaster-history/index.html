A Century of Disasters

125 years of the world's disasters — natural and man-made — and what the data says about the link between technology and staying alive. Built on EM-DAT via Our World in Data plus a hand-curated event catalog.

02 · The technology link

Deaths collapsed as warning technology arrived

Adjusted for population, dying in a disaster is about 46× less likely than in the 1920s. The diamonds under the bars are the technologies and institutions that did it — hover each one. Meanwhile the number of recorded disasters went up, not down.

03 · What kills, by era

The killers changed

Early-century mega-deaths came from floods and droughts — famine-scale events that modern logistics and forecasting ended. Earthquakes never went away, and extreme temperature is the one that's growing.

05 · The satellite view

What the satellites are watching

Every open event in NASA's EONET tracker — no basemap needed; the disasters draw the continents themselves. This is what "we see more disasters than ever" looks like: one agency's satellite feed logs thousands of events a year, most of which would never have entered a 1950s record.

06 · What the data says

Six findings

Sources. Aggregate series: Our World in Data — Natural Disasters (underlying data EM-DAT, CRED / UCLouvain), CC BY. Satellite events: NASA EONET via the Global Natural Calamities Dataset on Kaggle (CC0). Per-event catalog: hand-curated from public records; death tolls are best estimates and several (1931 China floods, Banqiao, the famines) are disputed by an order of magnitude. Population denominators: UN estimates by decade.

Scope. Wars are excluded. Epidemics and famines are included where EM-DAT-style catalogs include them — they are the century's biggest killers and the clearest proof that "disaster" is often a policy outcome, not just weather.
